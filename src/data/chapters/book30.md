The crowd was gathered, as usual, about the royal pair. 
The Queen regarded a guard, who held an axe of considerable size.  

“This will not mend his head,” she said.  

The guard, having no better instruction, continued to hold it.  

“I am not certain,” said the King, “that I have been punished enough.”
This sounded serious, though no one knew in what way.  

“I have been King for a great many years,” he went on, “and yet there has 
been neither correction nor any unrest.”  

“No,” said the Queen, “this will not do at all.”  

The Cat’s whiskers spread like rays of sunshine. When it yawned, 
the light caught upon its teeth, and several persons were obliged to blink.  

“A head,” it observed, “is best governed from within. It would be most exhausting 
to answer to every concern that is brought before it. As with all good councils, 
one will make some discontent and others jealous. That is the conclusion 
of all that is fair and just.”  

The King nodded. Her Royal Highness regarded him with some suspicion, but allowed the matter to rest.
